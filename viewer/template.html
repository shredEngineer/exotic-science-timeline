Nine centuries of anomaly claims, graded by evidence.

People have been reporting machines that run without fuel, weights that change on a scale, and heat that no chemistry accounts for — since 1150. This is the record of those reports, and of how well each one was documented.

It takes no position on whether any effect is real. Every entry is graded on six ordered axes by EQO, an open standard that classifies what was observed and grades how well it is documented — orthodox and heterodox cases in one vocabulary. It assigns coordinates, never verdicts.

Epistemic Quantization of Observables

The standard ↗
EQO

Epistemic Quantization of Observables is the open classification standard this record is built on. It gives every documented observation a phenomenon class, a physical context, the explanations on record for it, and a six-axis coordinate describing its evidence. Orthodox and heterodox cases share one vocabulary, and the standard forbids itself a credibility score — it assigns coordinates, never verdicts.

Every badge in the record links to the row below that defines it.

About this record

Provenance, stated up front

The corpus was compiled with the assistance of a large language model from publicly documented claims. Every record carries its own documentation and provenance grades, plus a separate confidence field for the compiler's own recall. Verify against primary sources before citing. This is not a caveat bolted on at the end — the instrument exists to make exactly this kind of grading explicit, and exempting itself would be incoherent.

Named people

Historical figures appear by the record only, with observation kept separate from interpretation. Where a legal outcome is reported but could not be checked against a primary source, the entry says so rather than asserting it. A build fails automatically if any entry alleges wrongdoing by a named person without a source.
